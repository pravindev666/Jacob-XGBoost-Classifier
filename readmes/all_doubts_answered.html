What is XGBoost actually doing? Is it just math?
XGBoost is building a forest of decision trees. Each tree is a series of IF-THEN questions:

Tree 1 asks: "Is RSI below 40?" → YES → "Is VIX below 18?" → YES → "Predict UP (60% confident)"
Tree 2 asks: "Is MACD positive?" → YES → "Is FII buying?" → YES → "Predict UP (65% confident)"
... × 300 trees

Final answer = weighted average of all 300 trees. If 210 trees say UP and 90 say DOWN → 70% probability UP → Confidence score 70%.
It's not magic. It's 300 analysts asking different questions and voting. The genius is that each new tree corrects the mistakes of all previous trees (that's the "boosting" in XGBoost).

This is the most important concept. Let's do the math:

58% win rate, 8 trades/month:
Wins: 4.64 trades × ₹2,250 avg = +₹10,440
Losses: 3.36 trades × ₹2,000 avg = -₹6,720
Net: +₹3,720/month

Add credit spread wins (70% win rate) and you reach ₹5,000+/month.

Renaissance Technologies makes BILLIONS with 50.75% accuracy. The casino makes billions with 52% win rate on roulette. You don't need to be right 9 out of 10 times. You need to be right slightly more than half the time, consistently, with proper sizing.
The formula is: Edge × Position Size × Number of Trades = Profit. A small edge repeated 100 times beats a big edge used once.
What is "overfitting" and how do I know if my model has it?
Overfitting = the model memorised the training data instead of learning patterns.

It's like a student who memorises 500 past exam questions word for word. They score 100% on a practice test with those exact questions. But the real exam has new questions → they fail.

How to detect it:
— Train accuracy = 85%, Val accuracy = 56% → OVERFITTING (big gap)
— Train accuracy = 65%, Val accuracy = 61% → HEALTHY (small gap)

How to fix it:
1. Reduce max_depth from 5 to 3
2. Reduce n_estimators from 300 to 150
3. Increase subsample from 0.8 to 0.9
4. Add more training data (go back to 2010 if you have it)
What does "early stopping = 30" mean? Why does XGBoost stop before 300 trees?
Early stopping saves you from overfitting automatically.

XGBoost trains tree by tree. After each tree, it checks: "Did my validation accuracy improve?" If it didn't improve for 30 trees in a row, it stops — even if you asked for 300 trees.

Example: Tree 147 gives best validation accuracy. Trees 148–177 all make it slightly worse. At tree 177, XGBoost says "ok I peaked at 147, stopping here, rolling back to tree 147."

This is why you might see "Best iteration: 183" in the output even though you set 300 estimators.
Think of it like baking a cake. The recipe says 40 minutes but you check every 5 minutes. At 30 minutes it's perfect. At 35 it's slightly burnt. At 40 it's ruined. Early stopping is the oven alarm that goes off when the cake is at its best.

What happens when I click "Load Data & Train XGBoost"?
Here's exactly what runs, step by step:

Step 1 (0-30%): Reads all your CSVs from data/ folder. Merges nifty_daily + vix_daily + pcr + fii + sp500 + bank_nifty into one big table with ~6,000 rows.

Step 2 (30-40%): Runs features.py — calculates RSI, MACD, VIX spike, BB width, all 55+ features. Creates a "target" column = 1 if next day close > today close, else 0.

Step 3 (40-55%): Splits data by year. Scales features using StandardScaler (mean=0, std=1) fitted ONLY on train data.

Step 4 (55-85%): XGBoost trains 300 trees one by one. After each tree it checks validation accuracy. Stops early if no improvement for 30 rounds.

Step 5 (85-100%): Evaluates on test set (data it never saw). Shows accuracy, confusion matrix, feature importance. Predicts today's direction.

The trained model is saved to Streamlit session state. This means it's available on Signal Engine page instantly — no retraining needed until you restart the app.
The sidebar has "India VIX" and "Nifty Spot" sliders — what are those for?
Those are global live parameters that feed into multiple pages simultaneously:

India VIX slider: Sets the current VIX for the Signal Engine page (overrides auto-loaded value if you want to simulate different scenarios). Also controls the VIX regime display on Dashboard.

Nifty Spot: Used by Signal Engine to calculate exact strike prices (e.g. "ATM" = nearest 100 to spot). Also used by Strategy Guide page to draw payoff diagrams at the correct price level.

In normal use: leave these at default (they auto-load from your latest CSV data). Use them to ask "what if VIX was 25?" scenarios.
Why does the Signal Engine sometimes say "No Trade" even when the model is confident?
Two reasons:

Reason 1 — Confidence below 55%: The model's probability is close to 50% (e.g. 62% UP). That converts to confidence = |0.62 - 0.50| × 200 = 24%. Below the 55% threshold → No Trade. The model doesn't have a strong enough edge today.

Reason 2 — VIX override: Even if confidence is 72%, if VIX is 22, buying a CE/PE is expensive and risky. The system might downgrade to "Bear Call Spread" instead of "No Trade" — but it will never buy options in VIX > 20 unless confidence is very high.

No Trade is the right answer ~40% of days. That's the system protecting your capital. Missing a trade costs ₹0. Taking a bad trade costs ₹2,000.
